"I Almost Fell in Love with a Machine”: Speaking with Computers Affects Closeness and Self-disclosure

Qian Yu, Tonya Nguyen, Soravis Prakkamakul, Niloufar Salehi


While all of our participants were anonymous, one of the participants happened to be a friend. By coincidence, I saw their Instagram post explaining that they “almost fell in love with a machine named Lee,” our conversational agent. Permission to use photo granted by subject.



Motivation

Listening and speaking are tied to human experiences of closeness and trust.

As voice interfaces gain mainstream popularity, we ask: is our relationship with technology that speaks with us fundamentally different from technology we use to read and type? In particular, will we disclose more about ourselves to computers that speak to us and listen to our answer?

We examine this question through a controlled experiment where a conversational agent asked participants closeness-generating questions common in social psychology through either text-based or voice-based interfaces.

We found that people skipped more invasive questions when reading-typing compared to speaking-listening. Surprisingly, typing in their answers seemed to increased the propensity for self-disclosure. This research has implications for the future design of voice-based conversational agents and deepens concerns of user privacy.

1.3 Interface using text as output, human speech as input and 1.4 Interface using text as input and output

Reflections

On my first CHI Submission

In Fall 2018, I joined Professor Niloufar Salehi's graduate class Research Topics in HCI. My project focused on investigating the affective effects of speaking and listening to vocal user interfaces. This project revealed to me how powerful interface design can be. Changing input and output modality affected user behavior by increasing the propensity of self-disclosure, which may create privacy implications for VUIs.

Thank you so much to my teammates Soravis and Qian, two masters students, who were like mentors to me. This was my first HCI project and publication. I'm sure it will not be my last!
